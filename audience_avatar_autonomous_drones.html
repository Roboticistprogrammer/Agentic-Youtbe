Challenges & pain points

The documentation wall

PX4, ROS2, Isaac Sim, and ArduPilot all have extensive docs — but they assume you already know what you don't know. He can install everything and still have no idea which piece is missing or in the wrong order. The docs explain what things do, not why they fail.

The demo gap

He finds a YouTube tutorial, follows every step, and it still doesn't work — because the video was recorded on a different OS version, firmware, or hardware setup. He can't diagnose the divergence. He's stuck not because he lacks intelligence, but because he lacks the mental model to locate the error.

The pace problem

Getting a single example running can take days or weeks. By the time something works, he's lost the thread of what he was trying to learn. Progress feels invisible and the learning curve feels vertical, even for a capable engineer.

Isolation

He may be the only person in his lab working on this exact stack. There's no peer to ask "have you seen this error before?" He relies entirely on GitHub issues, Discord servers, and Reddit threads written by strangers who may have a completely different setup.

Fears

Fear of irrelevance

The field is moving fast. If he spends six months in setup hell, he worries others are already implementing features he hasn't even read about yet. The fear isn't failure — it's being permanently behind.

Fear of being a fraud

He has the degree, but can he actually build things? This is the quiet anxiety behind every failed tutorial. He knows the theory but doubts his ability to translate it into something real that works in front of other people.

Fear of wrong choices

PX4 or ArduPilot? ROS2 or a custom stack? Isaac Sim or Gazebo? Every tool choice feels irreversible. He's afraid of investing months into a path that turns out to be the wrong one for the job market or his thesis.

Fear of no entry point

The deepest fear: that the industry is a closed loop — you need experience to get hired, but you can only get experience if you're hired. He worries there's no legitimate path in for someone without a lab or a senior engineer guiding them.

Objections

"Is this content actually up to date?"

His biggest trust barrier. He's been burned by tutorials that use deprecated APIs or outdated firmware. He'll check the upload date and the comments for broken-setup complaints before committing to a series.

"Does this person actually build things, or just explain them?"

He's skeptical of pure educators. He wants evidence that the creator has shipped something real — a live flight demo, a published paper, a GitHub repo with recent commits. Credentials from a distance don't move him as much as footage of something flying.

"Will this work on my exact setup?"

He knows his hardware and OS combination might differ. He wants the creator to explicitly state versions, environments, and known incompatibilities — not assume a clean universal setup. The more specific the creator is about context, the more trust he extends.

Positioning & language

"I can't clearly understand what is going on in the flight controller during this phase."

"I got it installed but I have no idea why it works now and it didn't before."

"The docs say to do X but they don't explain what X actually does under the hood."

He speaks in system terms: firmware, middleware, stack, pipeline, planner, controller, topic, node, simulation environment. He doesn't say "it's confusing" — he says "the integration between the offboard controller and the flight stack is unclear." Matching his technical register builds instant credibility. Talking down to him loses it permanently.

Emotional landscape

He operates in a loop of high ambition → grinding frustration → brief euphoria → renewed ambition. The euphoria hits when something finally works, and it's powerful enough to pull him through weeks of friction. That moment — when the drone arms, the simulation runs cleanly, the position hold holds — is almost addictive. Your videos need to give him a version of that feeling even before he opens his terminal.

He experiences slow progress as a personal indictment rather than a structural problem. He compares himself to people who seem to ship things faster and internalizes the gap as a talent deficit. He needs content that normalizes the struggle without being condescending — a creator who says "this part trips everyone up, here's exactly why" is giving him permission to not feel stupid.

Anti-avatar profile

Who they are

Mid-20s to early 40s, any background except relevant engineering. Could be a hobbyist, a business person who "wants to build a drone startup," a content creator who wants drone footage, or a self-taught coder who thinks programming experience alone transfers. They found the channel through a recommended video or a trending term like "AI drone" or "autonomous flight."

What they want

A copy-paste solution. A five-minute setup. A dramatic result they can screenshot and post. They're not interested in what PX4's state machine is doing — they want the drone to follow them on a hike. The depth you offer is not a feature to them; it's a barrier.

Their behavior in your comments

"Can you make a video that explains this without all the technical stuff?" or "This is too complicated, can you simplify?" They leave early in long videos, skip the fundamentals section, then ask questions in the comments that were answered in the part they skipped. They blame the content when the real mismatch is the audience.

Why they're genuinely incompatible

Simplifying for them means stripping the value that makes your content worth watching for Amir. You can't serve both audiences without destroying the channel for the one who needs it most. The anti-avatar's presence in your comments can also signal to Amir that the channel is "too basic" — which is the opposite of what you want.
